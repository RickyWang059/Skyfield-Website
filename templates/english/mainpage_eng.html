INTRODUCTION


Skyfield

As the sun sets and night descends, the stars compete for brilliance in the sky. This captivating scene has stirred the hearts and minds of poets and scholars since ancient times, evoking both melancholy and curiosity. What mysterious force governs the rhythmic cycle of day and night, orchestrating the rise and fall of the sun, moon, and stars? How deep is the abyss of the night sky? Where do we come from, and where are we headed? Do the heavens, the earth, and everything in between have a beginning and an end? These age-old questions, once confined to the realms of philosophy and religion, are gradually being unraveled through scientific inquiry as we uncover the mysteries of the cosmos...




Constellation

The term refers to a group of stars whose projected positions on the celestial sphere are close together. The delineation of constellations may vary across different civilizations and historical periods. Most modern constellations evolved from ancient Greek traditions and are precisely delineated into 88 constellations by the International Astronomical Union.









HISTORY


For the convenience of studying and observing the myriad of stars in the sky, people divided the celestial sphere into several regions, with each region forming a constellation. It's challenging to pinpoint exactly when humans began conceptualizing constellations, as this astronomical knowledge likely predates recorded history. The names of constellations may have originated from early seafaring sailors. The origins of constellations in different civilizations could be entirely different, but with the expansion and mutual influence of various civilizations, the cultural aspects of constellations have undergone a process of amalgamation.
