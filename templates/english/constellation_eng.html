Constellations refer to groups of stars in the sky. Since ancient times, humans have linked clusters of stars with characters or objects from their mythology, and these collective myths are known as constellations. The division and naming of constellations varied across different civilizations until 1930, when the International Astronomical Union standardized the complex divisions. They based this on the ancient Greek constellations passed down through the Middle Ages and divided the sky into 88 official constellations with precise boundaries, ensuring that most stars belong to a specific constellation.

In the three-dimensional universe, these stars do not necessarily have any actual relationship with each other. Even if they appear close to each other on the celestial sphere, they may be very far apart in reality. If we were located in another solar system within the Milky Way, the night sky we see would be completely different.





.

Significance and Application



The rising and setting of stars or constellations were often used for navigation and determining time in ancient times. The ancient Egyptians determined the beginning of the year by observing the heliacal rising of Sirius. In some regions, the ancient techniques of determining direction by observing stars are still preserved. Constellations and their literary symbolism frequently appear in the works of scholars and poets.

Although the importance of constellations has relatively diminished in modern times, they have not lost their charm for night sky enthusiasts. Through their captivating legends, constellations play an extremely important role in the popularization of astronomy. Whenever significant astronomical events occur, astronomers, science communicators, and the media often spark a wave of enthusiasm for astronomy in society, especially among young people.






.

Motion



The apparent motion of constellations with the celestial sphere is caused by the Earth's own movements, particularly its rotation and revolution. The Earth's rotation causes the background of the night sky to rotate around the celestial poles once every 24 hours. This daily motion makes the stars and constellations appear to rise in the east and set in the west. As the Earth orbits the Sun over the course of a year, the night sky changes gradually with the seasons. After a year, the night sky appears almost the same as it did the previous year. Diurnal Motion

Due to the Earth's rotation from west to east, celestial bodies such as the Sun, Moon, and many other planets appear to rise in the east and set in the west each day. They each move along curved paths of varying sizes in the sky. This regular motion of celestial bodies is known as "diurnal motion."

Observers at different latitudes on Earth see different portions of the celestial sphere at the same time, resulting in variations in the constellations and star clusters visible to them. For example, for an observer at latitude 25 degrees north, the path of star A remains visible throughout the entire day as it appears to move along the celestial equator. However, there are stars closer to the celestial South Pole that will always remain below the observer's horizon.



Annual Motion

In addition to the daily motion caused by the Earth's rotation, celestial bodies on the celestial sphere also exhibit an annual motion due to the Earth's orbit around the Sun. This annual motion, occurring over a period of one year, results in the appearance of different constellations at different times of the year on Earth, corresponding to the changing seasons.

Due to the simultaneous rotation of the Earth on its axis and its orbit around the Sun, the Earth completes its diurnal motion, or daily rotation, with respect to the Sun. In practice, this rotation exceeds 360 degrees by approximately 1 degree (as illustrated below), equivalent to about 4 minutes. This phenomenon causes a celestial body or constellation to rise approximately 4 minutes earlier each day. Consequently, different constellations are visible in the sky during different seasons.






.

Modern 88 Constellations



In 1930, the International Astronomical Union officially announced the internationally recognized scheme of 88 constellations, while simultaneously establishing the spring equinox point of 1875 and the equator as references. Based on the right ascension coordinates of the central points of constellations, the 88 constellations can be divided into spring, summer, autumn, and winter constellations. Similarly, based on the declination coordinates of the central points of constellations, the 88 constellations can be categorized as northern, equatorial, zodiacal, and southern constellations.

Additionally, constellations can also be grouped according to different regional collections. These constellation families are named after the most prominent constellations within each group, as well as after the zodiac, mythological regions, celestial aquaria, and the astronomers Johannes Bayer and Nicolas-Louis de Lacaille, who created the southern constellations.
